Generating realistic synthetic option prices requires implied volatility (IV) as an input, yet IV itself is derived from observed option prices, creating a circular dependency that limits synthetic data availability. In this study, we broke this circularity by developing a three-stage pipeline in which IV emerged as an output of a structural model of equity returns rather than as an exogenous input. A Jump Hidden Markov Model first generated multi-asset price paths with heavy tails, volatility clustering, and cross-asset dependence via a Student-$t$ copula. A modified Heston stochastic variance process then converted those price paths into implied volatility, where the mean-reversion target $\theta$ was driven by the HMM regime state, days to expiration, moneyness, and an aggregate market mood indicator derived endogenously from the fraction of tickers occupying tail states. A Cox--Ross--Rubinstein binomial tree priced American options with early exercise using the resulting IV. A central design choice was the equilibrium initialization $v_0 = \theta(s_0, \mathrm{DTE}, K/S_0, M_0)$, which gave each strike-expiration pair its own mean-reversion target so that the smile, skew, and term structure of the IV surface emerged automatically without external calibration data. We calibrated a five-parameter smile and term-structure function on semiconductor option chains spanning multiple expirations and demonstrated that the smile shape generalized across tickers with only a scalar level adjustment. The multi-expiration calibration revealed a U-shaped ATM term structure driven by gamma compression at short maturities and uncertainty premia at longer horizons, which the model captured through a quadratic extension to the log-DTE term. The framework is implemented as an open-source Julia package designed to serve as a synthetic data generator for downstream machine-learning and risk-analysis applications.
