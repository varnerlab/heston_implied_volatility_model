\section{Calibration and Validation}\label{sec:calibration}

\subsection{Data}

The empirical foundation of this study was a set of option chains for four semiconductor stocks: NVDA, AMD, MU, and INTC. All chains were single-date snapshots sourced from barchart.com (October--November 2025) with DTEs ranging from 41 to 80 days. We filtered each chain to moneyness $K/S \in [0.70, 1.30]$ and IV $< 200\%$ to exclude illiquid deep ITM/OTM contracts with unreliable IV. Table~\ref{tab:data} summarizes the dataset after filtering.

\subsection{Calibration procedure}

Since each chain was a single-date snapshot, all observations shared one HMM state $s_0$ and market mood $M_0$. The model IV at a given strike reduced to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibration_iv}
    \sigma_{\text{model}}(K) = \sqrt{\theta_{\text{base}} \cdot \psi(\text{DTE},\; K/S)}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_{\text{base}} = \theta_{s_0} \cdot (1 + \gamma M_0)$ absorbed the state and mood into a single scalar. We fixed $\beta_1 = 0.20$ (term-structure slope) because single-DTE snapshots cannot identify it; multi-expiration data from the same observation date would allow direct calibration of $\beta_1$ (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}). The remaining parameters $(\theta_{\text{base}}, \beta_2, \beta_3, \beta_4)$ were fitted by minimizing the mean squared IV error on NVDA:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:objective}
    \min_{\theta_{\text{base}}, \beta_2, \beta_3, \beta_4} \;\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(\sigma_{\text{model}}(K_i) - \text{IV}_{\text{market},i}\bigr)^2
\end{equation}
using Nelder--Mead optimization. We chose NVDA as the calibration ticker because it had the richest chain (142 contracts after filtering).

\subsection{Results}

The calibrated parameters are reported in Table~\ref{tab:params}. The negative $\beta_2$ produced put skew; the positive $\beta_4$ produced smile curvature; and the interaction term $\beta_3$ captured how the skew flattened at longer maturities. The calibration achieved an RMSE of 1.16\% IV on NVDA.

\subsection{Cross-ticker validation}

To determine whether the smile \emph{shape} generalized across tickers, we applied the $(\beta_1, \beta_2, \beta_3, \beta_4)$ calibrated on NVDA to AMD, MU, and INTC, re-optimizing only the scalar $\theta_{\text{base}}$ per ticker. Table~\ref{tab:rmse} reports the root-mean-square IV error; Figure~\ref{fig:nvda_fit} shows the NVDA calibration fit; and Figure~\ref{fig:all_tickers} compares the model smile to market data across all four tickers by moneyness.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Cross-ticker RMSE (\% IV). The smile shape $(\beta_2, \beta_3, \beta_4)$ was shared from NVDA; only $\theta_{\text{base}}$ was re-fitted per ticker.}\label{tab:rmse}
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
Ticker & $\theta_{\text{base}}$ (IV level) & RMSE (\% IV) \\
\midrule
NVDA (calibration) & 28.9\% & 1.16 \\
AMD  & 38.7\% & 2.34 \\
MU   & 49.0\% & 3.99 \\
INTC & 35.9\% & 4.65 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The NVDA fit confirmed that four parameters sufficed to capture the smile shape within the tradeable moneyness range (RMSE = 1.16\% IV). The cross-ticker validation revealed that the shared smile shape transferred well to AMD (2.34\%) and reasonably to MU (3.99\%) and INTC (4.65\%). The higher RMSE for MU and INTC reflected differences in skew steepness across these names---MU exhibited a steeper put skew than NVDA, while INTC had a flatter OTM call wing---suggesting that per-ticker $\beta$ calibration would further reduce errors when multi-strike data is available. The residual analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:residuals}) confirmed that the errors were concentrated in the wings ($K/S < 0.85$ and $K/S > 1.15$), with near-ATM residuals below 1\% IV for all tickers.
