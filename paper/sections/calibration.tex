\section{Calibration and Validation}\label{sec:calibration}

\subsection{Data}

The empirical foundation of this study was a set of option chains for four semiconductor stocks: NVDA, AMD, MU, and INTC. All chains were single-date snapshots sourced from barchart.com (October--November 2025) with DTEs ranging from 41 to 80 days. We filtered each chain to moneyness $K/S \in [0.70, 1.30]$ and IV $< 200\%$ to exclude illiquid deep ITM/OTM contracts with unreliable IV (Table~\ref{tab:data}).

\subsection{Calibration procedure}

Since each chain was a single-date snapshot, all observations shared one HMM state $s_0$ and market mood $M_0$. The model IV at a given strike reduced to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibration_iv}
    \sigma_{\text{model}}(K) = \sqrt{\theta_{\text{base}} \cdot \psi(\text{DTE},\; K/S)}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_{\text{base}} = \theta_{s_0} \cdot (1 + \gamma M_0)$ absorbed the state and mood into a single scalar. We fixed $\beta_1 = 0.20$ (term-structure slope) because single-DTE snapshots cannot identify it; multi-expiration data from the same observation date allowed direct calibration of $\beta_1$, as described below. The remaining parameters $(\theta_{\text{base}}, \beta_2, \beta_3, \beta_4)$ were fitted by minimizing the mean squared IV error on NVDA:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:objective}
    \min_{\theta_{\text{base}}, \beta_2, \beta_3, \beta_4} \;\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(\sigma_{\text{model}}(K_i) - \text{IV}_{\text{market},i}\bigr)^2
\end{equation}
using Nelder--Mead optimization. We chose NVDA as the calibration ticker because it had the richest chain (142 contracts after filtering).

\subsection{Single-DTE results and cross-ticker validation}

The calibrated parameters (Table~\ref{tab:params}) produced the expected smile features: the negative $\beta_2$ generated put skew, the positive $\beta_4$ generated smile curvature, and the interaction term $\beta_3$ captured how the skew flattened at longer maturities. The calibration achieved an RMSE of 1.16\% IV on NVDA.

The NVDA fit confirmed that four parameters sufficed to capture the smile shape within the tradeable moneyness range (RMSE = 1.16\% IV; Figure~\ref{fig:nvda_fit}). The cross-ticker validation revealed that the shared smile shape transferred well to AMD (2.34\%) and reasonably to MU (3.99\%) and INTC (4.65\%; Table~\ref{tab:rmse}; Figure~\ref{fig:all_tickers}). The higher RMSE for MU and INTC reflected differences in skew steepness across these names; MU exhibited a steeper put skew than NVDA, while INTC had a flatter OTM call wing, suggesting that per-ticker $\beta$ calibration would further reduce errors when multi-strike data is available. The residual analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:residuals}) confirmed that the errors were concentrated in the wings ($K/S < 0.85$ and $K/S > 1.15$), with near-ATM residuals below 1\% IV for all tickers.

\subsection{Multi-expiration term structure calibration}

Having validated the smile shape across tickers, we next addressed the term-structure limitation by collecting NVDA call and put chains at seven expirations on the same date (April 6, 2026): 0, 2, 4, 7, 14, 25, and 46 DTE, with NVDA trading near \$176.70. The single-DTE calibration above required fixing $\beta_1 = 0.20$; this cross-sectional snapshot provided the multi-expiration data needed to identify $\beta_1$ directly, yielding 73 observations after filtering to moneyness $K/S \in [0.90, 1.10]$ with positive volume.

The ATM IV exhibited a U-shaped term structure (Figure~\ref{fig:atm_term}): elevated at 0~DTE ($\sim$45\%) due to gamma compression in expiring options, dropping to a trough near 7--8~DTE ($\sim$32\%), then rising to $\sim$41\% at 46~DTE. A single power-law $\beta_1 \cdot \ln(\text{DTE})$ could not capture this shape. We extended $\psi$ with a quadratic DTE term:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:psi_extended}
    \psi = \exp\bigl(\beta_1 \ln \tau + \beta_2 \ln m + \beta_3 \ln \tau \cdot \ln m + \beta_4 (\ln m)^2 + \beta_5 (\ln \tau)^2\bigr)
\end{equation}
where $\tau = \max(\text{DTE}, 1)$ and $m = K/S$. The six parameters $(\sigma_{\text{base}}, \beta_1, \ldots, \beta_5)$ were fitted by minimizing mean squared IV error across all seven expirations simultaneously.

\centering
\caption{Calibrated $\psi$ parameters from the multi-expiration NVDA dataset. The quadratic DTE term $\beta_5$ captured the U-shaped ATM term structure.}\label{tab:term_params}
\begin{tabular}{llr}
\toprule
Parameter & Interpretation & Value \\
\midrule
$\sigma_{\text{base}}$ & Base ATM IV level & 45.0\% \\
$\beta_1$ & DTE decay           & $-0.622$ \\
$\beta_2$ & Skew (asymmetry)    & $-8.448$ \\
$\beta_3$ & DTE $\times$ skew   & 1.617 \\
$\beta_4$ & Smile curvature     & $-1.242$ \\
$\beta_5$ & DTE curvature       & 0.150 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The negative $\beta_1$ combined with positive $\beta_5$ produced the U-shaped ATM curve (Table~\ref{tab:term_params}): at short maturities the linear term dominated (IV decayed rapidly from the 0~DTE spike), while at longer maturities the quadratic term bent the curve upward. The trough occurred near DTE~$\approx 8$, where IV reached 32.5\%, a 1.38$\times$ ratio between the 0~DTE peak and the trough (Figure~\ref{fig:atm_term}). The skew parameters ($\beta_2 = -8.45$, $\beta_3 = 1.62$) confirmed that the volatility skew steepened at shorter maturities and flattened as DTE increased, consistent with the concentration of crash risk premia in near-term options (Figure~\ref{fig:iv_surface}). The overall fit achieved an RMSE of 2.31\% IV across all seven expirations. The fit was tightest at intermediate and long maturities (7--46~DTE: RMSE $< 1.3$\%), with the largest residuals at 0~DTE (RMSE = 6.57\%) where the gamma-compressed smile shape deviated from the log-moneyness functional form. The 0~DTE level bias was eliminated ($-0.1$\%), confirming that the U-shaped term structure was captured correctly even though the extreme smile curvature at expiration remained a source of scatter (Figure~\ref{fig:iv_heatmap}).
