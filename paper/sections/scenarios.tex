\section{Scenario Generation}\label{sec:scenarios}

To demonstrate the framework as a synthetic data generator, we simulated 500 forward paths for NVDA over 80 trading days using the sector-specific neural $\psi_{\text{NN}}$ calibrated in Section~\ref{sec:ladder} for the technology sector, the corresponding $\theta_{\text{NVDA}}$ level, and a JumpHMM marginal model with 50 states and tuned jump parameters. The tail states were assigned elevated $\theta$ values ($2\times$ baseline for bearish tail states, $1.5\times$ for bullish tail states), and the mood sensitivity was set to $\gamma = 0.5$. The mean IV started near the calibrated ATM level and exhibited gentle term-structure decay as the contract approached expiration, consistent with the positive $\beta_1 = 0.20$ used in the single-DTE calibration (Figure~\ref{fig:iv_paths}). Individual paths showed stochastic fluctuations driven by the vol-of-vol parameter $\sigma_v$, with occasional spikes when the HMM transitioned into tail states. The 5th to 95th percentile envelope widened over time, reflecting the accumulation of variance process noise, and the market ATM IV of 44.6\% sat within the model's distributional range and confirmed that the calibrated parameters produced IV dynamics centered on the observed level.

To translate these IV dynamics into contract valuations, we computed American option prices across five strike levels at $t = 5$ days. The price distributions exhibited realistic properties (Table~\ref{tab:prices}): OTM puts (strike $= 0.90S$) had a mean price of \$12.59 with a 90\% confidence interval of [\$5.6, \$21.1]; ATM calls were approximately $2\times$ more expensive than 10\%-OTM calls; and the confidence intervals widened for contracts farther from the money. The market mood mechanism produced a natural partition of scenarios into calm ($M_t = 0$) and stressed ($M_t > 0$) paths. In multi-asset simulations with five tickers, stressed scenarios (where multiple tickers simultaneously occupied tail HMM states) produced systematically higher option prices. OTM puts were $1.83\times$ more expensive in stressed scenarios relative to calm scenarios (strike $= 0.95S$: \$6.42 compared with \$3.51), while deep OTM weekly puts showed a $4.33\times$ stress premium. This asymmetry, with puts repricing more sharply than calls under stress, was consistent with the crash-protection premium observed in equity options markets~\cite{bates2000} and emerged naturally from the JumpHMM's negative jump bias ($p_{\text{neg}} = 0.52$) propagating through the $\theta$-function.
