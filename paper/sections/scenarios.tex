\section{Scenario Generation}\label{sec:scenarios}

To demonstrate the framework as a synthetic data generator, we simulated 500 forward paths for NVDA over 80 trading days using the calibrated parameters from Section~\ref{sec:calibration} and a JumpHMM marginal model with 50 states and tuned jump parameters. The tail states were assigned elevated $\theta$ values ($2\times$ baseline for bearish tail states, $1.5\times$ for bullish tail states), and the mood sensitivity was set to $\gamma = 0.5$.

\subsection{Forward IV dynamics}

Figure~\ref{fig:iv_paths} shows the evolution of implied volatility across the 500 scenarios. The mean IV started near the calibrated ATM level and exhibited gentle term-structure decay as the contract approached expiration---consistent with the positive $\beta_1 = 0.20$ (contango). Individual paths showed stochastic fluctuations driven by the vol-of-vol parameter $\sigma_v$, with occasional spikes when the HMM transitioned into tail states. The 5th--95th percentile envelope widened over time, reflecting the accumulation of variance process noise. The market ATM IV of 44.6\% sat within the model's distributional range, confirming that the calibrated parameters produced IV dynamics centered on the observed level.

\subsection{Option price distributions}

Table~\ref{tab:prices} reports the distribution of American option prices across scenarios, evaluated at day 5 of the forward simulation. The price distributions exhibited realistic properties: OTM puts (strike $= 0.90S$) had a mean price of \$12.59 with a 90\% confidence interval of [\$5.6, \$21.1]; ATM calls were approximately $2\times$ more expensive than 10\%-OTM calls; and the confidence intervals widened for contracts farther from the money. These properties are consistent with the behavior of real option markets.

\subsection{Tail event analysis}

The market mood mechanism produced a natural partition of scenarios into calm ($M_t = 0$) and stressed ($M_t > 0$) paths. In multi-asset simulations with five tickers, stressed scenarios---where multiple tickers simultaneously occupied tail HMM states---produced systematically higher option prices. OTM puts were $1.83\times$ more expensive in stressed vs.\ calm scenarios (strike $= 0.95S$: \$6.42 vs.\ \$3.51), while deep OTM weekly puts showed a $4.33\times$ stress premium. This asymmetry---puts repricing more sharply than calls under stress---is consistent with the crash-protection premium observed in equity options markets and emerged naturally from the JumpHMM's negative jump bias ($p_{\text{neg}} = 0.52$) propagating through the $\theta$-function.
